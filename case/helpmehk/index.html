Design Process

A bit of background

As a working adult now, I have been taking more and more short weekend getaways. I usually don't buy a mobile or 3g plan, considering how short my trips were. So I would have no mobile connection or 3G plan, in a country where I didnâ€™t speak the language: Essentially screwed during emergencies.

I also got into a nasty cycling accident. All I wanted to do was get to a hospital as soon as possible: but I had to sit around checking my Google Maps for clinics, which gave me results for dental clinics, plastic surgeons, TCM etc, not the kind I wanted. After narrowing down the closest clinics, I had to manually check the opening hours, give them a call and figuring out if they were open.

The Idea

I couldn't really find out any apps that served during emergency situations. So I decided to make one that would help tourists during emergencies.

I wanted an app that would show you the nearest emergency services based on your location. Essentially, you could open the app, and boom, you would know where to go in cases of emergency.

I targetted this app primarly towards tourists, who might not be familiar with a city's emergency servies. A quick survey of 200 respondents revealed that 88% of them do not do any research on emergency services (police stations, hospitals, embassies) before they travel.

The survey also revealed that more than 90% of tourists did not purchase a mobile data plan when they were travelling. This meant that all emergency data had to be accessible offline.

The Design Process

The first part was getting the data about emergency locations. Luckily, the HK government had recently began an OpenData scheme, so information on emergency services was easily available.

Another great advantage of this data was that it was regularly updated by the HK, so all information would be current. We devised a system where the app would download new data everytime there was a change, and store all this info offline.

Next, I picked the types of emergency service information to be displayed. Police and medical services were the most obvious ones. As we were catering to tourists, consulate information also became a category.

I drew a couple of quick pencil sketches on how we can display this data to our users.

7 initial sketches on displaying the data. Option 6 & 7 were rejected as they prioritized location, without providing any other categorization. But the ability to select categories between vastly different emergency services was important.

Option 2 provided a more "top-down" view of the information, narrowing from category, to the location list, and finally the details of each location. This natural progression and overview it provided made the app easier to understand.

A more detailed drawing of the Option 2 revealed a problem with it: The funnel used to provide information bit by bit to users meant that I had to present more screens to the user. Each screen presents decision making, as well as uncertainty about what the next screen was. My goal for this app was to reduce decision making to as little as possible, so I wanted to push this further.

The process that the user has to take before reaching the final screen. Users had to make it through 3 screens before any information was given to them.

Option 5 in this case presented a good balance between categorizing information and making information easily accessible. The headers helped to seperate categories and providing summarized information on the first screen itself made decision making a lot easier.

Wireframe and colored version of the idea. The colored headers help to categorize the information groups, and the summary made information more easily accessible.

This design presented a problem. Firstly there was no indication from the design that you were able to click on each section and pull out more detailed information about each place.

So this was changed into a more card-like design, which indicates without instruction that there will be more information available upon pressing the card.

Cards made it obvious that the each card, when clicked will pull up more information. But users tended to scroll up and down to find more information.

The card design, when tested showed another issue. When people viewed the app, because of the card design, the tended to scroll up and down. There was no indication on the design that they needed to swipe left/right to get more information about each screen.

So we then thought of using cards that were peeking out on both sides to signal scrolling. This was a intuitive way of suggesting the ability to view multiple locations within each category.

The final design, with cards peeking out from left and right to suggest sideways scrolling.

Next I had to decide how I could display detailed information, such as opening hours and phone numers. I tried to display all information about each place in one shot. So as the app opened, you would have the fully detailed information. So each location card would contain all information so everything was available without any button presses.

However, what happened was, there was an overwhelming amount information presented to the user. Users were at an early stage of decision making (narrowing down to their top 1 or 2 choices). Presenting them with information that they did not need at this stage. They only needed the nitty gritty details once their decisions had been made, ie the ability to drill down for more information of maybe their top two choices.

Providing too much information in an effort to reduce decision making actually made the app more confusing. A good balance between presenting everything vs decision making had to be struck.

So instead, I stuck to my earlier design. So, once you clicked on each card, it would provide you more detailed information about each location. This would be key information like full address, opening hours, a directions option that led you to google maps, and finally a translation option.

Detailed view of each location

I had got this idea from a recent trip to thailand. As the app will be primarily for tourists, who will not speak the local language, showing location details in mandarin will aid in taxis, or asking for directions.

Translations which were useful in crossing the language barrier!
